# ASD-STE100 Issue 9 Writing Rules (Distilled)

Distilled from ASD-STE100 Issue 9 (2025-01-15).

## Text types

Classify every passage before you rewrite it. The rules differ:

| Type               | What it is                                 | Verb form                  | Sentence limit |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------ | -------------------------- | -------------- |
| Procedural         | Instructions, work steps                   | Imperative                 | 20 words       |
| Descriptive        | Descriptions, reports, explanations, notes | Declarative, no imperative | 25 words       |
| Safety instruction | Warnings and cautions inside procedures    | Imperative                 | 20 words       |

Notes inside procedures are descriptive text (25 words per sentence).

## Words (Section 1)

- **1.1** Use only: words approved in the dictionary, technical nouns, technical verbs.
- **1.2** Use approved words only as their specified part of speech. "Test" and "check" are approved as nouns, not verbs: write "Do a test for leaks", not "Test for leaks".
- **1.3** Use approved words only with their approved meanings. Many approved words have restricted meanings:
  - "follow" = come after, go after. For instructions, use "obey": "Obey the safety instructions."
  - "wear" (v) = become damaged by friction. For clothing, use "put on" or "use".
  - "about" = concerned with. For quantity, use "approximately".
  - "see" = perceive with the eyes only. For verification, use "make sure that".
  - "above"/"below" = physical position only. For limits, use "more than"/"less than".
  - "turn" = move around an axis. For state changes, write "The color of the indicator changes to green."
- **1.4** Use only the approved forms of verbs and adjectives given in the dictionary.
- **1.14** Use American English spelling (fiber, color), unless official directives say differently. Never change spelling inside quoted text.

## Technical nouns (Rules 1.5 thru 1.11)

A technical noun (TN) is a noun term for a specified concept in a subject field. The dictionary deliberately excludes them. A word that is not approved is still usable if it fits one of these 22 categories (Rule 1.5, examples in parentheses):

1. Official parts information (bolt, oil seal, transceiver)
2. Vehicles or machines, and locations on them (fuselage, cockpit, engine room)
3. Tools and support equipment (torque wrench, test rig, jack)
4. Materials, consumables, unwanted material (grease, sealant, debris)
5. Facilities, infrastructure, logistics (hangar, storage, packaging)
6. Systems, components, circuits, their functions and configurations (latch, standby mode, exhaust)
7. Mathematical, scientific, engineering terms and formulas (torque, ratio, voltage, curve)
8. Navigation and geographic terms (altitude, heading, north)
9. Numbers, units of measurement, time (10 kg, half, winter)
10. Quoted text: placards, labels, markings, display text (EXIT sign, ON position)
11. Roles, individuals, organizations, geopolitical entities (captain, FAA, manufacturer)
12. Parts of the body (skin, lung, hand)
13. Common personal effects, food, beverages (clothing, matches, coffee)
14. Medical terms (dizziness, irritation, pulse)
15. Official documents, parts of documentation, standards (chapter, Service Bulletin, checklist)
16. Environmental and operational conditions (humidity, turbulence, night)
17. Colors (red, gray; never comparative forms like "redder")
18. Damage terms (crack, corrosion, dent, scratch)
19. Computer science, information and communication technology (database, cursor, backup, firewall)
20. Civil and military operations (mission, deployment, customer support)
21. Law and regulations (contract, compliance, waiver)
22. Animals, plants, other life forms (insect, bacteria, bird)

Constraints:

- **1.6** A word that is not approved is usable only as a TN or part of a TN, and only in a context that fits its category. "Base" is a TN in "The base of the triangle" (category 7) but not in "the base of the unit" (write "bottom").
- **1.7** Do not use TNs as verbs: "Apply oil to the surfaces", not "Oil the surfaces". "Put clamps on the cable", not "Clamp the cable".
- **1.8** Use the TN your company, industry, or subject field approves. Do not "fix" parts of established technical names ("main landing gear" stays, even though "main" alone is not approved).
- **1.9** With no approved TN available, select one that is short (3 words or less) and easy to understand.
- **1.10** No regional, slang, or jargon words ("brick the router", "gear" for equipment).
- **1.11** One TN per item, used consistently through the whole text.

## Technical verbs (Rules 1.12 and 1.13)

A technical verb (TV) is a verb term for a specified process in a subject field. Four categories (Rule 1.12):

1. Manufacturing processes: remove material (drill, ream), add (insulate), attach (solder, weld), change properties (anneal, freeze), change surface (polish, plate), change shape (cast, extrude)
2. Computer processes and applications: input/output (click, enter, type), user interface (copy, paste, scroll, delete), system operations (boot, install, download, reboot)
3. Instructions for applicable subject fields: engineering/scientific (convert, radiate), medical (sterilize), civil/military (arm, fire), navigation (land, taxi, hover), automotive/railway (brake, accelerate), energy/oil/gas (compress, distill)
4. Law and regulations (comply with, notify, supersede)

Constraints:

- If an approved dictionary verb gives the instruction accurately, use it instead of a TV: "If you find broken wires" (not "detect"), but "The security scanner detects metal objects" (correct TV context).
- Prefer an approved verb plus a TN over a TV: "Apply grease to the fasteners", not "Grease the fasteners".
- **1.13** Do not use TVs as nouns: "Ream the hole to a 0.20-inch dimension", not "Give the hole 0.20-inch ream". The past participle as adjective is permitted ("the reamed hole").
- Technical verbs obey the same form and tense rules as approved verbs (Section 3).

## Multi-word nouns (Section 2)

- **2.1** Multi-word nouns: 3 words maximum. Break longer clusters with prepositions: "Calibration of the resistance of the runway light connection", not "Runway light connection resistance calibration".
- **2.2** An official technical noun longer than 3 words: write it in full the first time, then use a shorter form or its approved abbreviation, or hyphenate the words that act as one unit ("Main-gear-door retraction-winch handle"). Hyphenated groups count as one word. Never invent or remove hyphens in official technical nouns.

## Verbs (Section 3)

- **3.1** Use only the verb forms given in the dictionary.
- **3.2** Only these forms and tenses: infinitive, imperative, simple present, simple past, simple future, past participle as adjective. Never: perfect tenses ("has adjusted"), progressive ("is adjusting"), or other complex constructions.
- **3.3** The past participle is an adjective only: before a noun ("the disassembled unit") or after "be"/"become"/"stay".
- **3.4** No auxiliary-verb constructions: "You can adjust the volume control", not "The volume control can be adjusted". "Adjust the temperature", not "The temperature must be adjusted".
- **3.5** No "-ing" forms, except as (part of) a technical noun ("Cleaning", "air-conditioning system", "grinding wheel") and the few approved dictionary words: nouns lighting, opening, routing, servicing; adjectives mating, missing, remaining; pronoun something; preposition during. Rewrite the rest: "When you do this procedure", not "When you are doing this procedure".
- **3.6** Active voice, always in procedures, and in descriptive text unless the agent is unknown ("During transmission, the data was corrupted" is permitted). To convert passive: name the agent as subject, or use the imperative, or use "you"/"we" as subject.
- **3.7** Describe actions with verbs, not nominalizations: "Before you remove the unit", not "Before the removal of the unit".

## Sentences (Section 4)

- **4.1** Short, clear sentences. One topic per sentence in descriptive text. Be concrete: "Make sure that there are no leaks", not "No leaks are permitted". Do not keep abstract statements: "When the temperature increases, the cure time decreases", not "Different temperatures will change the cure time".
- **4.2** Do not omit words or use contractions. Keep subjects, verbs, articles: "If shims are installed, remove them", not "If installed, remove the shims". "Set the rotary switch to INPUT", not "Rotary switch to INPUT". "do not", never "don't".
- **4.3** Use a vertical list for complex text. Mechanics: colon at the end of the lead-in, each item starts with an uppercase letter, article before the noun where applicable, period only after items that are full sentences and always after the last item, no commas or semicolons at item ends, one level of structure, each item must connect grammatically to the lead-in. In safety instructions, repeat "DO NOT" in each item instead of factoring it into the lead-in. Do not mix procedural and descriptive items in one list.
- **4.4** Connect related sentences with connecting words and phrases: "and", "but", "then", "thus", "as a result", "at the same time".
- **4.5** Use articles (the, a, an) and demonstrative adjectives (this, these) before nouns. Do not drop them for brevity: "Turn the shaft assembly". No articles for general statements ("Solvents can cause damage to paint") or before a noun with an alphanumeric identifier ("Tag circuit breaker 36L7").

## Procedural writing (Section 5)

- **5.1** Maximum 20 words per sentence, including warnings and cautions. Notes: 25.
- **5.2** One instruction per sentence, shown in sequenced work steps. More than one action is permitted only when the actions occur at the same time ("Hold the panel in its open position and install the fastener") or when a result or limit follows the action immediately ("Measure the leakage from the outlet port. The leakage must not be more than 0.5 cc/minute.").
- **5.3** Instructions in the imperative: "Continue the test", not "The test can be continued". No "must" before an imperative except in safety instructions or important conditions.
- **5.4** Condition first, then a comma, then the command: "When the light comes on, set the switch to NORMAL." "If the CSD does not operate correctly, disconnect it from the gearbox."
- **5.5** Notes give information only, never instructions, requirements, or limits. An instruction hiding in a note becomes a work step. Safety-relevant information in a note becomes a warning or caution. Limits and tolerances go directly after the action in the work step.

## Descriptive writing (Section 6)

- **6.1** Give information gradually, one subject per sentence.
- **6.2** Repeat the same key words and phrases to connect sentences. Do not vary terminology for style.
- **6.3** Maximum 25 words per sentence.
- **6.4** Group related information in paragraphs. Start each paragraph with a topic sentence.
- **6.5** One topic per paragraph.
- **6.6** Maximum 6 sentences per paragraph.

## Safety instructions (Section 7)

A warning tells the reader there is a risk of injury or death. A caution tells the reader there is a risk of damage to objects. Both risks together: use a warning.

- **7.1** Identify the level of risk with the applicable word (warning, caution).
- **7.2** Start with a clear command or, when the reader must know it first, a condition: "WHILE YOU USE THE SPRAY PAINT, POINT THE SPRAY AWAY FROM YOUR FACE."
- **7.3** Explain the risk or possible result: "THE SPRAY PAINT CAN CAUSE INJURY TO YOUR EYES."

Template: `[CONDITION,] COMMAND. RISK OR RESULT.` STE gives no formatting rules (uppercase is convention, not requirement).

## Punctuation and word count (Section 8)

- **8.1** All standard punctuation except the semicolon. Replace a semicolon with two sentences.
- **8.2** Hyphenate words that are directly related: multi-word adjectives before a noun (low-altitude flight, quick-release fastener), two-word numbers (forty-seven), letter/number plus noun (O-ring, 3-prong connector), compound verbs (heat-treat, short-circuit), prefix vowel plus root vowel (pre-amplifier, de-icing).
- **8.3** Parentheses are permitted for: references to illustrations or text, item identifiers, work-step identifiers, abbreviations, singular and plural at the same time ("test(s)"), explanations, alternatives.

Word counting:

- **8.4** In a vertical list, the colon ends a sentence. The lead-in and each item get their own word budget (20 procedural, 25 descriptive).
- **8.5** Text in parentheses counts as one word of the sentence, and its content counts separately as its own sentence.
- **8.6** Count as one word each: numbers, a number with its unit ("10 °C"), abbreviations (NASA, VPN), alphanumeric identifiers (36L7, No. 1), quoted text (a formula, "Service Overview"), titles/headings/placard and label text, proper nouns of people, organizations, geopolitical entities.
- **8.7** Hyphenated words count as one word.

## Writing practices (Section 9)

- **9.1** When a word-for-word replacement changes the meaning, is a different part of speech, or produces bad English, use a different sentence construction. "Lift the seat until it is away from the track locks" for "so that it clears the track locks" ("clean", the listed alternative for "clear", would be wrong). Think about the intended meaning first, then rebuild.
- **9.2** Use each approved word correctly: with its approved meaning, in its approved context, as its approved part of speech. "Do work with cleaning agents" ("work" is a noun), "with the aid of" ("help" is a verb), "cause damage to" ("damage" is a noun).
- **9.3** No phrasal verbs: "extinguish", not "put out"; "release", not "give off". The dictionary approves only a few, with restricted meanings (for example "put on", "come on", "go off").
- **9.4** Consistent style: the same work step always gets the same wording, the same item always gets the same name.

## General recommendations (GR-1 thru GR-8, not rules)

- GR-1: Keep the conjunction "that": "Make sure that the valve is open."
- GR-2: Watch "with" for ambiguity ("Install the panel with the green fasteners" has 3 readings). Put the primary action verb first: "Seal the opening with tool TS9867", not "Use tool TS9867 to seal the opening."
- GR-3: If a pronoun can refer to more than one noun, repeat the noun.
- GR-4: If "this" can refer to more than one thing, restate the context.
- GR-5: Watch false friends from the writer's native language.
- GR-6: No Latin abbreviations (e.g., i.e., etc.). Write "for example", "that is", or restructure.
- GR-7: Gender-neutral language only. "He" and "she" are not in the dictionary.
- GR-8: The possessive ('s) is permitted but use it only when you are sure it is correct.

## Word-selection algorithm

For any word you are not sure about:

1. Look it up in the dictionary (protocol below).
2. Entry in UPPERCASE: approved. Confirm your use matches the approved meaning AND part of speech. If not, treat it as not approved for this use.
3. Entry in lowercase: not approved. Use the UPPERCASE alternatives given. If the alternative has the same part of speech and preserves meaning, replace word for word. Otherwise rebuild the sentence (Rule 9.1).
4. No entry: usable only if it fits a technical noun or technical verb category and belongs to the text's subject field. Otherwise find an approved synonym or rebuild the sentence.
5. An alternative marked (TN) or (TV) means the replacement is a technical noun or technical verb, not a dictionary word.
6. When no approved verb fits an action, "do a/the <noun>" with the action as a noun usually works: "Do a fluorescent-penetrant inspection", "Do a check of the battery".

## Dictionary lookup protocol

The full dictionary (875 approved words, 1274 unapproved words with alternatives) is in `ASD-STE100_ISSUE9.txt`. Entries start at the beginning of a line as `WORD (pos)` in UPPERCASE (approved) or `word (pos)` in lowercase (not approved), followed by the approved meaning or the UPPERCASE alternatives, an STE example, and a non-STE example.

Look up a word with the Grep tool: pattern `^word`, case-insensitive (`-i`), with line numbers (`-n`) and about 8 lines of context (`-A 8`). The case of the match tells you its status. Then:

- Dictionary entries sit after line 6160 (the end of the `List of approved verbs`). Matches at lower line numbers come from the issue change log and the indexes: ignore them.
- A word can appear as more than one entry (approved as one part of speech, not approved as another). Read every match in the dictionary region.
- The "help" notes inside entries add restrictions ("Use this word for safety instructions only", "For other meanings, use: ..."). Obey them.

If `ASD-STE100_ISSUE9.txt` is not in this folder (it is gitignored), work from this file alone: the approved-verbs list, the recurring-errors table, and the restricted meanings above. Name the words you could not verify in the delivery report. To restore full-fidelity lookups, get the specification per [README.md](README.md).

| ----------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| acceptable (adj)  | PERMITTED (adj)                            |
| alternate (adj)   | ALTERNATIVE (adj)                          |
| any (adj)         | None or a different sentence construction  |
| avoid (v)         | PREVENT (v)                                |
| both (adj)        | THE TWO (TN)                               |
| check (v)         | CHECK (n): "do a check of"                 |
| complete (adj)    | COMPLETED (adj)                            |
| cover (v)         | COVER (TN): "put a cover on"               |

| ensure (v)        | MAKE SURE (v)                              |

| portion (n)       | PART (n)                                   |
| press (v)         | PUSH (v)                                   |
| reach (v)         | GET (v)                                    |

| secure (v)        | ATTACH (v), SAFETY (v)                     |
| shall (v)         | MUST (v)                                   |
| should (v)        | MUST (v)                                   |
| since (conj)      | BECAUSE (conj)                             |
| test (v)          | TEST (n): "do a test of"                   |

| under (prep)      | BELOW (prep), IN (prep), LESS THAN         |
| using (v)         | USE (v), WITH (prep)                       |

Meaning trap in this table: "should" and "may" map to MUST and CAN, but a blind swap upgrades advice to obligation or permission to capability. When the source clearly gives a recommendation, keep it one: "We recommend that you ..." (RECOMMEND is approved). Confirm intent before you harden it.
